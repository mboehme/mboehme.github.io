This manifesto is a pledge to lead my research group with a consistent reproducibility policy.
  1. I will educate my graduate students about sound empirical analysis and reproducibility.
  2. We will make all our source code publicly available upon acceptance (as far as funder allows).
  3. We will make all papers available by Green Open Access (as far as publisher allows).
  4. We will share data, scripts, and figures for the main results under CC-BY.
  5. We will add a "Reproducibility" declaration at the end of each paper.

Software Engineering Reproducibility Manifesto (SERM)

Are we in a reproducibility crisis? Many scientific disciplines are. Earlier research findings cannot be reproduced or are not reproducible in the first place. However, the software engineering community has the unique opportunity to provide almost all artefacts that are required for successfully reproducing the entire set of a paper's findings. All our artefacts are digital. For instance, there are some excellent guidelines on how to effectively package all artefacts required for reproducing the findings. The ROSE Festival celebrates replication in software engineering. The ACM provides artifact badges for repeatable, replicable, and reproducible. ESEC/FSE'19 had Open Science guidelines, and a dedicated Open Science chair (in addition to the Artefact Evaluation Committee chairs).

This manifesto is a first grassroots effort to inspire collective action from the local level.

The reproducibility of research is at the very heart of the scientific method. In specific instances, it is fairly reasonable to avoid sharing data, scripts, and tools, e.g., if the authors wish to develop and commercialize the evaluated prototype or if the authors are bound by non-disclosure agreements. In general, however, we should strive for a scientific discourse that is open and inclusive. We should be allowed to inspect and build upon each others findings. If a central claim in the paper is supported only by empirical evidence, we should be able to provide that empirical evidence (within reason). If we are uncomfortable sharing a research prototype, what does it mean for the results in the experimental section? There is no need to protect. Science is not a competition.
